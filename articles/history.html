The Story Every Turk Carries

Five dates live in every Turkish head: 1071, 1453, 1915, 1919, 1923. Learn the story that connects them and you'll understand the flags, the portraits on every wall, and half of what your hosts are proud of before they say a word.

Nine centuries in one line: nomads → empire → collapse → republic. The two red dots are the ones carved deepest.

Prologue: written in stone, on horseback

The word "Türk" first appears carved on stones standing in the wind of central Mongolia — the Orkhon inscriptions, raised in the 730s by the Göktürk khaganate, the first state we know that called itself Turkish. Every Turkish schoolbook starts here: a people of the Central Asian steppe — horse-breeders, archers, empire-builders with an alphabet of their own — long before they ever saw the land now called Türkiye.

Over centuries the Turkic tribes drifted west, met Islam around the 10th century (the Seljuk dynasty converted and then championed it), and kept moving toward the richest, most contested real estate of the medieval world: Anatolia — already layered with Hittites, Troy, Greeks, Romans, and Byzantium. Today's Turks are heirs to all of it, and know it: Göbekli Tepe, in Türkiye's southeast, is the oldest known monumental temple on Earth — raised around 9600 BC, seven thousand years before the pyramids.

1071 — the door opens

On 26 August 1071, near the town of Manzikert (Malazgirt) north of Lake Van, the Seljuk sultan Alp Arslan met the Byzantine emperor Romanos IV Diogenes in battle. Chroniclers say Alp Arslan dressed that morning in white — his own burial shroud — telling his men that if he fell, they should bury him where he lay. He did not fall. The Byzantine army shattered, and for the first time in history a Roman emperor was taken prisoner on the battlefield.

What happened next is the part Turks like best: Alp Arslan treated Romanos as a royal guest, dined with him for a week, and released him. The chivalry is the point of the story as it's told — strength and magnanimity together. Strategically, Manzikert broke the Byzantine grip on Anatolia; Turkic tribes poured in, and within a decade there was a "Sultanate of Rum" — of Rome — ruling from Konya. 1071 is taught as the birth certificate of Turkish Anatolia. Politicians of every party invoke it to this day.

The Seljuk capital Konya became a world capital of mysticism: Rumi — Mevlana to Turks — wrote and taught there in the 1200s, and his order gave the world the whirling dervishes. When a Turk quotes poetry at dinner, odds are it's Rumi or Yunus Emre.
The long walk west (schematic): steppe → Persia → Anatolia. One battle in 1071 turned a frontier into a homeland.

Osman's dream and the empire of the in-between

When the Mongols smashed the Seljuks in the 1240s, Anatolia splintered into small warrior principalities — beyliks. One of the smallest, wedged right against the Byzantine border, was led by a chief named Osman. Legend — recorded later, and every Turk knows it — says Osman dreamed of a moon descending into his chest, and from it a tree growing to shade the whole world. His name became the dynasty's: Osmanlı — the Ottomans.

Their genius was position and pragmatism: sitting on the seam between two worlds, they absorbed from both — Byzantine administration, Persian court culture, Islamic law, and a startling openness to talent from anywhere. The elite janissary corps and half the grand viziers were recruited from Christian-born boys (the devşirme system) raised at the palace — brutal by modern lights, but it built the most meritocratic ruling machine of its era.

1453 — the twenty-one-year-old and the ships that sailed over land

Constantinople in 1453 was a shrunken city of maybe 50,000 behind the mightiest walls ever built — walls that had repelled twenty-odd sieges over a thousand years. Sultan Mehmed II was 21 years old. His court advised patience; he declared, as Turkish schoolbooks quote it: "Either I take Istanbul, or Istanbul takes me."

He brought innovations: the largest cannon on Earth (cast by a Hungarian founder, Urban — its barrel could fit a man crawling), and when a great chain blocked the Golden Horn to his fleet, he did the thing every Turkish child can narrate — in one night his men hauled some seventy ships out of the water and dragged them overland on greased timber rails, over the hills of Galata, back into the sea behind the chain. The defenders woke to an enemy fleet inside their harbour. On 29 May 1453, the walls broke. The Roman Empire — 1,500 years of it, counting from Augustus — ended that morning.

Mehmed, thereafter Fatih ("the Conqueror" — you'll see the name on bridges, mosques, a whole district), rode to Hagia Sophia and converted it to a mosque, but also promptly repopulated the city with Greeks, Armenians and Jews under protection, and styled himself Kayser-i Rum — Caesar of Rome. The Ottomans never saw themselves as destroyers of Rome; they saw themselves as its next chapter.

Fatih also brought Italian masters to his court — Gentile Bellini painted his portrait (it hangs in London's National Gallery). A sultan commissioning Renaissance portraiture two decades after the conquest tells you what kind of empire this was going to be.

The peak: Süleyman, the lawgiver in love

The 1500s were the Ottoman century. Selim I doubled the empire in eight years and took the caliphate itself — from then on the sultan was also the leader of Sunni Islam. His son Süleyman reigned forty-six years (1520–1566), besieged Vienna, and ruled from Budapest to Baghdad, Algiers to Mecca. Europe called him "the Magnificent"; Turks call him Kanuni — the Lawgiver — because what they remember is the legal code, the administration, the architecture (his architect Sinan built over 300 works; the Süleymaniye still crowns Istanbul's skyline).

Educated Turks also know his softer scandal: Süleyman fell in love with a slave girl from the Ukrainian steppes — Hürrem, Europe's "Roxelana" — freed her, and married her, something sultans simply did not do. He wrote her love poetry under the pen-name Muhibbî ("the lover"). Their letters survive. Their story is a national TV obsession to this day.

Ottoman gifts you use without knowing: tulips went from Istanbul's court gardens to Holland in the 1550s (triggering tulip mania), coffee reached Europe through Ottoman trade and the coffeehouses of Istanbul (opened 1550s — the world's first cafés), and Mozart's "Rondo alla Turca" imitates the janissary mehter — the oldest military marching band in the world, which still performs in Istanbul daily.
Six centuries in one curve: three of rise, three of retreat. The retreat is the part that explains modern Turkey's reflexes.

The long retreat — and why it still matters

After a second failed siege of Vienna (1683 — legend says the fleeing army's coffee sacks stocked Vienna's first cafés), the empire began a slow, humiliating, two-century retreat. The 19th century brought frantic modernisation (the Tanzimat reforms), crushing debt to European banks, and a nickname coined in European chancelleries that still stings: "the sick man of Europe."

Understanding this period explains something real about the country you're visiting: the deep Turkish sensitivity to foreign condescension and foreign interference is not paranoia; it's memory. The Ottoman state spent its last century being carved, indebted, and lectured by the great powers. Which brings us to the moment the carving knife came out fully.

1915 — Çanakkale: the wound that became a birth

In the First World War the Ottomans joined Germany, and in 1915 Britain and France sent the largest armada yet assembled to force the Dardanelles strait — Çanakkale — take Istanbul, and knock the empire out. Every Turk knows the slogan of what happened instead: "Çanakkale geçilmez" — Çanakkale cannot be passed.

Nine months of trench warfare on the Gallipoli peninsula killed or wounded roughly half a million men on both sides — Ottoman losses alone approached a quarter of a million. Out of the inferno rose an unknown 34-year-old lieutenant-colonel, Mustafa Kemal, who at the critical moment told his 57th Regiment: "I am not ordering you to attack. I am ordering you to die. In the time it takes us to die, other troops and commanders can take our place." The regiment was wiped out. The line held. The Allies withdrew.

Gallipoli is sacred ground to three nations — Turkey, Australia and New Zealand (the Anzacs landed there). Words of reconciliation attributed to Atatürk in 1934 — "your sons... having lost their lives on this land, have become our sons as well" — are inscribed at the battlefield, and every April 25 thousands of Australians and New Zealanders travel there for the dawn service. Enemies became, genuinely, friends.

1919–1923 — the resurrection

The empire lost the war, and the victors' Treaty of Sèvres (1920) did what Turks had feared: partitioned Anatolia itself between Greek, Italian, French, British and Armenian zones — the homeland reduced to a rump. Sèvres is still a live word in Turkish politics, shorthand for "they will carve us if we're weak."

On 19 May 1919, Mustafa Kemal — the hero of Gallipoli — landed at the Black Sea port of Samsun, ostensibly to supervise disarmament, actually to organise resistance. That date is now a national holiday, and when officialdom later required a birthday from him, he chose it: 19 May. The message — my life began with the nation's — is perfectly in character.

The War of Independence that followed (1919–1922) against the occupying and invading powers culminated in the great counter-offensive of 30 August 1922 (Victory Day, another holiday) and the recapture of İzmir. The Treaty of Lausanne (1923) tore up Sèvres and recognised full sovereignty. And then, on 29 October 1923 — Republic Day — the Grand National Assembly in Ankara (deliberately not imperial Istanbul) proclaimed the Republic of Türkiye, with Mustafa Kemal as its first president.

The reforms: rebuilding a nation's software

What followed was one of history's most radical peacetime transformations — every educated Turk can list these:

The alphabet you've been reading in this course — the perfectly phonetic one that let you pronounce Turkish in eight minutes — is itself a monument of this story. It was designed in 1928, and its regularity is no accident: it was built so a whole nation could learn to read fast. Literacy roughly quadrupled within two decades.

9:05, November 10

Atatürk died in Dolmabahçe Palace, Istanbul, on 10 November 1938, at 9:05 in the morning. Once a year, at that exact minute, sirens sound across Türkiye and the country stops — traffic halts on bridges, pedestrians stand still, ferries blast their horns on the Bosphorus. If you're ever there for it, stand still too; nothing you do in Türkiye will be noticed more warmly.

His mausoleum in Ankara, Anıtkabir, receives millions of visitors a year. His portrait hangs in every office you will enter, his signature adorns cars and forearms, and his statue stands in every town square. The reverence is not imposed nostalgia — for most Turks he is, simply, the reason there is a Türkiye at all. His most-quoted line hangs in classrooms nationwide: "Ne mutlu Türküm diyene" — "How happy is the one who says: I am a Turk."

What to do with all this at dinner

The practical payoff
  • Know the two red dates — 1453 and 1923 — and who Fatih, Kanuni and Atatürk are. Mentioning that you read about the ships dragged overland, or that Turkish women voted before French women did, will genuinely delight your hosts.
  • National holidays decode the calendar: Apr 23 (children/parliament), May 19 (youth/Atatürk's landing), Aug 30 (victory), Oct 29 (Republic). Offices close; flags multiply.
  • Çanakkale and the War of Independence are sacred, not sad, topics — asking sincere questions about them is welcomed. The 1915 Armenian tragedy, by contrast, is the single most contested topic in the country: not dinner conversation.
  • The through-line to remember: steppe riders → empire of Rome's heirs → carved by great powers → resurrected by one general. Pride and vigilance, in one story. That's the character of the country you're doing business with.
