Doing Business in Turkey: The Unwritten Rules

Everything here is the operating manual behind the phrases you've been learning — how trust is built, how deals are really made, and why the tea matters more than the contract.

Rule zero: the relationship IS the deal

The single most useful thing to understand about Turkish business culture is that it runs on a different trust architecture than Northern Europe or North America. In London or Berlin, trust is institutional: you trust the contract, the courts, the process — so you can do serious business with a stranger on day one. Turkey runs on relationship trust: people extend real trust to those inside their circle — family, hometown, school friends, proven partners — and are politely guarded with everyone else until you've earned your way in.

This isn't a flaw; it's a rational adaptation. Turkey's commercial history is long (the Grand Bazaar has been trading continuously since 1461), but its institutional enforcement is slow — commercial court cases can take years. When the referee is slow, you learn to play only with people you know. Hence: the first meeting is not about your proposal. It's about you.

In the World Values Survey, only around one Turk in ten agrees that "most people can be trusted" — among the lower scores in Europe (Nordic countries score six times higher). But studies of in-group loyalty show the inverse: obligations to family, friends, and established partners are honoured with an intensity that surprises Westerners. Low trust in strangers, deep trust inside the circle — that's the whole game in one statistic.

So… can you trust them?

Here's the honest answer you asked for, without flattery or cynicism.

Inside a relationship, Turkish business partners are among the most loyal you will find. The concept of söz — one's given word — carries genuine moral weight. A partner who has shared meals with you, met your colleagues, done a successful first deal, will often go far beyond the letter of any agreement: covering your mistake, rushing an order, defending you to others. Hospitality obligations are near-sacred; a guest is close to inviolable.

Outside a relationship, assume commercial self-interest and verify. You are, at first, an outsider — and an outsider on a short trip, which in any bazaar on Earth means: tourist pricing, optimistic promises, and "yes" answers that mean "I'd like that to be true." None of this is uniquely Turkish; it's what relationship-trust cultures look like from the outside. The error Westerners make is binary thinking — either "everyone's honest" or "everyone's a crook." The reality: trustworthiness is earned and reciprocal, and the system tells you exactly how to earn it (show up, share tea, come back, start small).

Practical calibration
  • Build the relationship AND paper the deal. Not either/or. A written contract won't offend anyone — Turkish firms use them heavily among themselves — but understand it's seen as a photo of today's understanding, not scripture. Renegotiation requests when circumstances change are normal, not betrayal; respond calmly and trade something for something.
  • Start with a small pilot. One contained deal executed well converts you from stranger to insider faster than any due-diligence report.
  • Verify credentials quietly. Chamber-of-commerce registration (every company has a registry number), references from mutual contacts. Asking around is normal here — the circle IS the credit bureau.
  • A "yes" is data, not a commitment. The commitment is the follow-up: a date, a signature, a deposit, an introduction to the boss.

Reading what they actually said

Turkish communication is high-context and face-protective. Blunt refusal is felt as rude, so "no" arrives dressed as something else. Your decoder ring:

They sayIt usually means
Bakarız — "we'll see"Probably no, said kindly
İnşallah — "God willing"Anywhere from "genuinely hope so" to "don't count on it" — tone and context decide
Zor — "it's difficult"No — but persuadable if you solve the difficulty
A long silence, a subject changeNo, and pressing will cost you face
Tamam, tamam + a date + a nameAn actual yes — specifics are the tell

The mirror rule: never deliver your "no" bluntly either, and never criticise anyone in front of others — not their staff, not their competitor, not the waiter. Public embarrassment ends relationships here quietly and permanently. Deliver hard messages privately, softened, and ideally packaged with a way out.

The meeting, minute by minute

  • Names: first name + Bey (men) / Hanım (women) — Ahmet Bey, Ayşe Hanım. You'll likely be "Reza Bey" within the hour; it's warmth, not presumption. Academic and professional titles (Doktor, Mühendis — engineer) are used proudly; use them if you know them.
  • Greetings: firm handshake with everyone in the room, most senior first, eye contact. Some observant Muslims prefer not to shake hands with the opposite sex — if someone places their hand on their chest instead, mirror it warmly; it's respect, not rejection.
  • The tea is not optional. Çay arrives unbidden and refusing it is refusing the relationship — accept even if you take one sip. Some 96% of Turks drink tea daily; it's the highest per-capita tea consumption on the planet (yes, above Britain). Business begins when the second tea arrives.
  • Small talk first — and actually mean it. Family, football (pick a diplomatic answer: "I hear Galatasaray–Fenerbahçe is the greatest derby in Europe" pleases everyone), food, your impressions of Turkey (be generous, be specific). Fifteen minutes is normal; being visibly impatient to "get down to business" reads as untrustworthy — someone who won't invest in the relationship won't honour it either.
  • Hierarchy is real. Companies are typically top-down; the most senior person may speak least and decide everything. Address arguments to the room but conclusions to the boss. If the boss isn't in the room, you're in a scouting meeting — valuable, but nothing will be decided.
  • Time is elastic — asymmetrically. You should be punctual; they may not be, especially in Istanbul, where traffic is a universally accepted force majeure. Build slack into your schedule and never show irritation at a 20-minute wait that ends in a three-hour warm meeting.

Negotiation: it's a sport, and they respect players

Bargaining in Turkey is not conflict — it's a ritual of mutual respect with eight centuries of bazaar craft behind it. Paying the first price marks you as a mark; negotiating well marks you as someone worth dealing with.

  • Expect padding. First offers leave room — substantial in the bazaar (a third to a half), smaller but real in B2B (often 10–25% depending on sector). A first quote is an invitation, not an insult.
  • Never open with your best number. If you do, you'll still be asked to move, and you'll have nowhere to go. Leave yourself concessions to give — they are the currency of respect. Each one you grant should be visibly painful and traded for something.
  • Patience is leverage. The side that must close today loses. Deadlines you reveal will be used; deadlines you invent for them ("my board meets Thursday") work better.
  • The relationship discount is real. Prices genuinely drop as warmth rises — over tea, over a meal, on the second visit. This is why rushing costs actual money.
  • Walking away works — if done warmly. A regretful, friendly exit ("the price is beyond me, but thank you for the tea, the shop is beautiful") triggers more final offers than any argument. You can always come back; you cannot come back from a hostile exit.
  • Emotion is part of the theatre. Mild theatrical dismay ("Çok pahalı! — with a smile) is expected. Genuine anger, sarcasm, or humiliating comparisons are not. Hard on the numbers, soft on the person — always.
  • Get the final version in writing, same day. After a warm session, understandings drift with the best of intentions. A same-day friendly email — "wonderful meeting, capturing what we agreed" — is standard practice and offends no one.
The after-signing surprise It is not rare for a signed deal to be reopened later — costs changed, the lira moved (a genuine and constant factor in Turkish business life), the boss reconsidered. This feels like bad faith to contract cultures; here it's viewed as adapting the partnership to reality. Stay calm, express disappointment privately, and negotiate the reopening as a trade: "we can revisit price if we extend the term." Fighting the principle of reopening is a lost battle; trading within it is the game.

The table, the bill, the gifts

  • The bill fight is choreography. Your host will pay, and will physically resist your attempts. Attempt anyway — twice, sincerely — then thank warmly and declare you host the next one (Bir dahaki sefere ben!). Splitting the bill is alien in hosted settings.
  • Alcohol: follow, don't lead. Turkey holds everything from rakı-at-every-dinner secular business culture to strictly observant teetotal firms. Let the host order first; never push drinks; never be the drunkest person at the table (a lasting reputation killer).
  • Compliment the food by name. Çok lezzetli and Elinize sağlık (to a home cook or proud restaurateur) buy more goodwill than any business concession.
  • Gifts: modest and thoughtful beats expensive — something from your country or city works perfectly. Avoid extravagance (it embarrasses and obligates). If invited to a home: sweets/baklava, quality chocolate, or flowers; avoid alcohol unless you're certain of the household.
  • Ramadan awareness: if your trip overlaps, don't schedule working lunches with observant counterparts; an invitation to an iftar dinner is an honour — accept.

The three untouchables

Turks will debate almost anything with gusto — except these, where a foreigner has nothing to win:

  • Atatürk. Genuinely revered across most of society, and literally protected: Law 5816 (1951) makes insulting his memory a criminal offence. Sincere admiration, on the other hand, opens doors — see the history article.
  • Politics, the Kurdish question, Armenia, Cyprus. Deep, painful, polarising. If a counterpart raises them, listen respectfully, ask gentle questions, offer no verdicts.
  • Comparisons that sting. Don't compare Turkey unfavourably to Greece, don't call Istanbul "Constantinople" jokingly, don't be surprised aloud that things are "so modern." Turks are simultaneously fiercely proud and sensitive to condescension — the safest and truest stance is respect without astonishment.
